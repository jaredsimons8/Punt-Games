Not Every Loss
Is Created Equal

If you watch baseball, you know not all 162 games are treated as must-wins. Some losses are the result of circumstance. Others are the result of a decision / a quiet, deliberate concession made before the first pitch is thrown. The Punt Index exists to tell the difference. It tracks when MLB managers field a demonstrably weakened lineup, and flags those games in real time / before the game starts, and throughout the game itself.

What Is a Punt Game?

A punt game is an MLB game where a manager has quietly decided / before the first pitch is thrown, or through decisions made during the game itself / that winning this particular game is not the priority. It's a calculated concession: rest the stars, save the arms, absorb the loss. The team never announces it. The box score won't say it. But the evidence is always there, if you know where to look. A punt game is still a punt game even if the team wins.

The Score

Every game is scored on a 0–80 scale across two independent tracks. The pre-game score evaluates lineup and pitching decisions before first pitch / if it reaches 46, the game is a confirmed punt and that verdict is locked. The in-game score evaluates behavioral signals during play and can independently confirm a punt even if pre-game looked clean. ≥ 46 = Punt · ≥ 28 = Review · Under 28 = Clean.

Why It Matters

Punt games are not rare. They happen throughout every season / quietly and without official acknowledgment. The team never announces it. The broadcaster rarely names it. Yet the evidence is in the lineup card and the pitching selection. Every punt game is a game that mattered in the standings where one side showed up and the other did not.

How It Works
01 / Data Source

All data originates from the official MLB Stats API / the same feed that underlies most professional baseball infrastructure. Every batting order, probable starter, series position, and in-game substitution is captured directly from the source, without interpretation or delay. The record is objective by design.

02 / The Baseline

Rather than relying on preseason projections, the system builds each team's baseline through observation / 60 games of actual lineup behavior before detection activates. That baseline is exponentially weighted toward the present: recent games carry full signal, older ones diminish. Trades, injuries, and role changes resolve themselves automatically. The algorithm tracks the roster as it actually exists, not as it was constructed. Once a team graduates from calibration, the algorithm retroactively scores their first 60 games using the now-established baseline / early-season punt games that couldn't be flagged in real time are recovered and added to the record. Retrograded games are marked with a ~ in the calendar.

03 / Lineup Evaluation

Each absence is assessed on its own terms. An injured player contributes nothing to the signal / the decision wasn't the manager's. A platoon situation is discounted based on handedness matchup. An unexplained healthy scratch carries full weight. The starting pitcher functions as an override: a team sending its ace to the mound cannot be punting, regardless of what the position player lineup looks like.

04 / In-Game Signals

Pre-game and in-game verdicts operate on independent tracks. A game confirmed as a punt before first pitch cannot be un-punted / the pre-game record is fixed. But a game that looked clean at lineup card can still cross the threshold during play: a manager deploying a position player on the mound, burning the bullpen before the fifth inning, or pulling everyday starters in a blowout each constitute behavioral evidence in their own right.

2 Starters Benched
+23
Moderate signal — not enough alone for most games, but reaches REVIEW territory combined with a series finale or fatigue context. Two everyday starters sitting is worth watching. For contenders (mot 0.9) this scores ~23 pts.
3 Starters Benched
+30
Strong signal. A contender (mot 0.9) with 3 starters out scores ~30 pts — reaches PUNT territory with a series finale or any fatigue context. A rebuilding team scores ~7. Three deliberate absences without injury explanation is a clear managerial decision.
4+ Starters Benched
+40
Near-certain punt for any contender. Four everyday starters sitting pushes a contender directly into PUNT territory on lineup signal alone — before any series position, fatigue, or standings context is added.
Spot Starter (≤2 GS, ERA > 5.00)
+5–6
An emergency or spot arm with ≤2 starts and a poor ERA (or no ERA yet) is itself a punt signal — good teams don't burn rotation depth on days they want to win. The ERA gate avoids penalizing legitimate prospects making debut starts. Season debuts before June are also exempt (likely a top prospect, not emergency arm).
ERA Gap ≥ 1.5 (opponent better)
+10
Clear pitching disadvantage vs opponent's starter. Uses raw ERA delta so elite rotations aren't penalized for having good arms. Weighted by start sample (≥4 starts = full weight), capped at 4.0 run difference.
Absolute ERA ≥ 6.00 (objectively poor)
+3–5
An objectively bad ERA regardless of the opponent — good teams don't deploy sub-replacement starters on competitive days. Fires only when the ERA is ≥6.00 with ≥3 starts (to avoid early-season noise). Capped at +6 pts; does not stack with the spot-starter signal.
Clinched / Eliminated
+22
Division clinched or mathematically eliminated / both remove competitive motivation. Clinched teams protect stars for the postseason; eliminated teams have nothing to fight for. Score breakdown shows the correct reason.
Series Finale (G3/3 or G4/4)
+9
G3 of a 3-game set where the punting team has already clinched or leads the series — the classic rest-day window (+4 more for 4-game series). Rubber games (tied series) and series the team is losing do not get this bonus. 2-game series are also excluded.
Doubleheader Nightcap
+14
Game 2 of a doubleheader is the single most reliable structural punt signal. Managers routinely rest their core lineup in the nightcap regardless of series context. Detected automatically from the MLB schedule API.
Back-to-Back Game
+3–5
Team played yesterday / rest priority rises, especially for contenders protecting everyday players. Motivation-scaled: a top contender (0.9×) gets +4.5 pts, a rebuilding team (0.2×) gets +1. Stacks with travel fatigue.
Cross-Country Travel
+2–4
Same-day cross-timezone travel is a structural rest signal. West→East is the harder direction (+5 × motivation) — the body clock runs 3 hours behind. East→West is softer (+3 × motivation) but still meaningful. Both require the team to have played the prior day (stacks with back-to-back).
Day Game After Night Game
+2–4
Less than ~16 hours between games. A night game followed by a 1pm start is a known recovery challenge. Managers routinely give aging veterans this game off. Motivation-scaled: contenders (+4.5 pts) protect their stars more aggressively than rebuilding teams (+1 pt).
Game 1 Suppression
×0.65
The ace almost always pitches Game 1 of any series. On top of the #1-starter reduction, G1 punt scores are multiplied down / managers simply do not punt opening games of a set.
Consecutive Games Streak (7+)
+2–8
Teams playing 7+ straight days accumulate fatigue that shows up in roster decisions. Scales with streak length — 7 days = ~2 pts, 10+ days = up to 9 pts × motivation. Helps detect end-of-long-homestand or late-road-trip rest days.
⚖️ Team Quality Multiplier
Every lineup and pitching signal is scaled by that team's punt motivation score (0.1–0.9). Contenders have high motivation / their star absences carry full weight. Rebuilding teams have low motivation / they're fielding their best available regardless. A 4-player absence from the Dodgers (0.9×) scores nearly 5× higher than the same absence from a rebuilding team (0.2×). The live standings API adjusts these tiers dynamically once teams have played 30+ games.
⬇ Ace Starter Reduction
A true ace on the mound signals the team is competing. Deploying ERA ≤ 3.00 (with ≥6 starts) reduces the entire punt score: ×0.75 for standard teams (25% reduction) and ×0.88 for clinched teams (12% reduction — clinched teams may still rest regulars even with their ace). Requires ≥6 starts to avoid noisy early-season ERA inflating or deflating the signal. A manager starting their best arm is making a competitive statement — lineup decisions alone cannot override that.
In-Game Evaluation (Independent Track · 0–80 scale)
These signals can independently confirm a punt even if the pre-game score was clean
Position Player Pitching
+45
The clearest concession signal in baseball. Using a position player on the mound only happens when a manager has completely surrendered. A single occurrence nearly reaches the PUNT threshold on its own.
Run Deficit Late (6th+)
+18–50
Down 5+ after the 6th. Scales with both deficit size and inning / a 10-run hole in the 9th is near certainty. Computed as (deficit−4) × inning weight.
Early Blowout (4th–5th)
+14
Down 5+ before the 6th. A blowout this early signals the game was effectively over before the manager could formally concede / strong behavioral indicator.
Bullpen Concession
+10–22
3+ pitching changes before the 5th inning. Burning the pen early signals the manager isn't managing to win / scales with how many changes occur.
Early Star Removal
+10–18
2+ offensive substitutions before the 7th inning in a blowout. Pulling everyday starters mid-game while still technically in reach is a clear concession signal.
Low-Leverage Arm, Close Game
+12–20
A mop-up reliever (ERA 6.50+) appearing while the game is within 3 runs / from the 5th inning onward. Bringing in garbage-time arms while the game is still competitive is a manager quietly waving the white flag. +12 for one such arm, +20 for two or more.
🔒 Pre-Game Lock + Independent In-Game Track
Pre-game and in-game are scored on separate 0–80 scales and evaluated independently.

Pre-game punt (≥ 46): Locked. A game confirmed before first pitch stays a punt regardless of in-game outcome. In-game signals add a small bonus but cannot reduce the classification.

In-game punt: A clean or review pre-game can still become a punt from in-game behavior alone. The badge reads PUNT · IN-GAME to distinguish it.

Blended (neither confirmed): Pre-game dominates early, shifting toward in-game as innings accumulate. Final games weight in-game at 35%.

🔄 Living Baseline / Adapts to Roster Changes
The algorithm compares each day's batting order against a recency-weighted composite baseline built from 60+ stored games. The last 20 games carry full weight, the 20 before that carry half, anything older carries quarter weight. Traded players fade out in ~3 weeks. Injured players returning earn their way back in as they accumulate appearances. The baseline always reflects who the team actually is right now / not who they were in April.
