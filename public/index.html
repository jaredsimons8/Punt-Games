Not Every Loss Is Created Equal

Some losses are the result of circumstance. Others are the result of a decision. The Punt Index exists to tell the difference — tracking when MLB managers, through a combination of pre-game choices and in-game behavior, effectively concede a game before the final out is recorded.

📋 What Is a Punt Game?

A punt game is not a single decision — it is a verdict reached through the full arc of a game. It begins with what a manager puts on the field: who is in the lineup, who is on the mound, and what the series context suggests about their intent. It continues through the game itself: how the bullpen is used, whether stars are removed before the seventh inning, whether a position player ends up pitching. A punt game can be strongly signaled before first pitch, confirmed during the game, or — in rare cases — only identifiable once the final out is recorded. The algorithm weighs all of it.

⚙️ How the Score Works

Every game is scored on a 0–10 scale — a weighted composite of two independent evaluations. The lineup and pitching score assesses the pre-game decision: who sat, who pitched, and what the series situation implied about motivation. The game-flow score assesses what unfolded during the game: run deficits in late innings, excessive early bullpen usage, position player pitching, and mid-game star removal. Neither score alone tells the full story. The final verdict blends both, shifting progressively toward game-flow data as more innings are completed — because a manager's choices during the game are often the clearest signal of all.

⚾ Why This Matters

Punt games are not rare. They happen throughout every season — quietly, deliberately, and without any official acknowledgment. The team never announces it. The broadcaster rarely names it. Yet the evidence is in the lineup card, the pitching selection, and the way the game is managed. Every punt game is a game that genuinely mattered in the standings where one side showed up to compete and the other did not. Over 162 games, these decisions accumulate. The Punt Index makes the invisible visible — a fan-built, original stat with no official MLB equivalent, tracking all 30 teams equally across an entire season.

How It Works

The Punt Index pulls live data directly from MLB's official API — the same source that powers the league's own platforms. Every lineup, every pitching decision, every in-game substitution runs through the same scoring system.

📡 The Data Source

Every batting order, every starting pitcher, every series position, every in-game substitution — all sourced from the official MLB Stats API, the same public feed that powers most professional baseball applications. This is not scraped data or estimation. When you hit Refresh, the system pulls real, confirmed data directly from MLB's servers for every game on the selected date, including live play-by-play for games in progress.

📊 Building the Baseline

Each team begins in calibration mode. Over the first 60 games, the system records the actual batting order every night — learning who the genuine everyday starters are through observed behavior rather than preseason projections. Once 60 games are stored, punt detection activates for that team.

The baseline never freezes. It continuously updates, weighting recent games more heavily — the last 20 games at full weight, the previous 20 at half weight, and older games at quarter weight. This means the system adapts organically: a player traded away fades out of the baseline within weeks. A player returning from injury — such as Ketel Marte, Blake Snell, or Jackson Holliday — earns their way back in as they accumulate appearances. The algorithm always reflects the team as it actually exists right now.

🔍 Evaluating the Lineup

The pre-game evaluation compares the day's batting order against each team's established norm. Not every absence carries the same weight. A player on the injured list contributes nothing to the score — their absence is not a managerial decision. A known platoon situation carries reduced weight. A healthy everyday starter sitting with no explanation carries full weight, further scaled by their recent appearance frequency.

The starting pitcher is evaluated separately and functions as a hard gate: if the ace is on the mound — defined as either a league-wide ace by FIP or the team's own season-best arm — the pre-game score is automatically set to zero. A manager sending their best pitcher does not concede the game before it starts.

⚡ Reading the Game

The in-game evaluation runs continuously for live and completed games. It looks for behavioral signals that confirm or contradict the pre-game picture. A large run deficit in the seventh inning suggests concession. Excessive pitching changes before the fifth suggest a manager who isn't committing to winning. A position player on the mound — the single clearest concession signal in baseball — adds significant weight regardless of the pre-game score. Removing everyday starters before the seventh in a still-winnable game tells a story the lineup card alone cannot.

These in-game signals are scored independently on their own 0–10 scale and blended into the final score using a weighted composite that shifts toward game-flow evidence as the game progresses. A game cannot be fully evaluated until it ends.

Series Clinched
+3.0
The single most reliable context signal. A series already decided removes almost all competitive motivation for a contender — and is the clearest situational setup for both pre-game and in-game concession behavior.
Last Game of Series
+1.0
Weak alone — G3 of a 3-game set always carries slightly more rest temptation. Only meaningful when combined with clinched status.
Last + Clinched Bonus
+0.5
The classic punt setup: last game of a series the team has already won. Small bonus stacked on top of the two individual signals.
ERA Confirms FIP
+0.5
When ERA and FIP both point to a weak pitching matchup, it adds a small confirmation bonus — both metrics telling the same story.
⚖️ Team Quality Multiplier
Every benched-starter signal is multiplied by that team's punt motivation score (0.4–0.9). Contenders with deep rosters and playoff pressure have high motivation to strategically rest stars — their absences carry full weight. Rebuilding teams have low motivation — they're playing their best available regardless. A 4-player absence from the Yankees (0.9×) scores much higher than the same absence from a rebuilding team (0.4×).
🚫 Ace Disqualifier — Lineup Score Zeroed
If a team is starting their ace, the lineup and pitching score is immediately set to zero. Two conditions trigger this: the starter's FIP ≤ 3.50 (a league-wide ace threshold), or their FIP falls within 0.40 of that team's own season-best. A manager who sends their best arm to the mound is making a competitive statement — regardless of who is in the batting order, the pitching decision alone disqualifies a lineup-based punt signal. In-game signals can still activate independently if the game unfolds that way.
⚡ Game-Flow Evaluation
Position Player Pitching
+3.0
The strongest in-game concession signal in baseball. Using a position player on the mound is a white flag — it only happens when a manager has completely surrendered the game.
Run Deficit Late (6th+)
+1.0–2.0
Down 5+ runs after the 6th inning. Weight scales with how late the game is — a 6-run deficit in the 9th is a harder concession signal than the same deficit in the 6th.
Bullpen Concession
+1.5
Three or more pitching changes before the 5th inning. This bulk/opener pattern signals the manager isn't committing to a game-winning effort from the start.
Early Star Removal
+1.0
Two or more offensive substitutions before the 7th inning while already losing by 4+. Pulling everyday starters mid-game in a still-winnable deficit is a clear concession signal.

🔄 Living Baseline — Adapts to Roster Changes
The algorithm compares each day's batting order against a recency-weighted composite baseline built from 60+ stored games. The last 20 games carry full weight, the 20 before that carry half weight, and anything older carries quarter weight. This means the baseline automatically adapts: a player traded away fades out within a few weeks. A player returning from injury earns their way back in as they accumulate appearances. The algorithm always reflects who the team actually is right now — not who they were in April.
