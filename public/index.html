Not Every Loss
Is Created Equal

If you watch baseball, you know not all 162 games are treated as must-wins. Some losses are the result of circumstance. Others are the result of a decision — a quiet, deliberate concession made before the first pitch is thrown. The Punt Index exists to tell the difference. It tracks when MLB managers field a demonstrably weakened lineup, and flags those games in real time — before the game starts, and throughout the game itself.

What Is a Punt Game?

A punt game is a major league game where a manager has made a deliberate, pre-meditated decision — before the first pitch is thrown, or through decisions made during the game — that winning this particular game is not the priority. It is a calculated concession: rest the stars, protect the arms, absorb the loss and prepare for what comes next. No manager announces a punt. The box score won't say it. But the evidence is always there, if you know where to look. A punt game is still a punt game even if the team wins.

The Score

Every game is scored on a 0–80 scale across two independent tracks. The pre-game score evaluates lineup and pitching decisions before first pitch / if it reaches 46, the game is a confirmed punt and that verdict is locked. The in-game score evaluates behavioral signals during play and can independently confirm a punt even if pre-game looked clean. ≥ 52 = Punt · ≥ 36 = Review · Under 36 = Clean.

Why It Matters

Punt games are not rare. They happen throughout every season — quietly and without official acknowledgment. No manager announces one. The broadcaster rarely names it. Yet the evidence is always in the lineup card and the pitching selection. Every punt game is a game that mattered in the standings where one side showed up and the other made a different decision.

How It Works
01 / Data Source

All data originates from the official MLB Stats API — the same feed that underlies most professional baseball infrastructure. Every batting order, probable starter, series position, and in-game substitution is captured directly from the source — without interpretation or delay. The record is objective by design.

02 / The Baseline

Rather than relying on preseason projections, the system builds each team's baseline through observation — 60 games of actual lineup behavior before detection activates. That baseline is recency-weighted toward the present: the last 20 games carry full signal, the 20 before that carry half weight, older games carry quarter weight. Trades, injuries, and role changes resolve themselves automatically. The algorithm tracks the roster as it actually exists, not as it was constructed. Once a team graduates from calibration, the algorithm retroactively scores their first 60 games using the now-established baseline — early-season punt games that couldn't be flagged in real time are recovered and added to the record. Retrograded games are marked with a ~ in the calendar.

03 / Lineup Evaluation

Each absence is assessed on its own terms. An injured player contributes nothing to the signal — the decision wasn't the manager's. A platoon situation is discounted based on handedness matchup. An unexplained healthy scratch carries full weight. The starting pitcher functions as an override: a team sending its ace to the mound cannot be punting, regardless of what the position player lineup looks like.

04 / In-Game Signals

Pre-game and in-game verdicts operate on independent tracks. A game confirmed as a punt before first pitch cannot be un-punted — the pre-game record is fixed. But a game that looked clean at lineup card can still cross the threshold during play: a manager deploying a position player on the mound, burning the bullpen before the fifth inning, or pulling everyday starters in a blowout each constitute independent behavioral evidence. A game that looked clean at first pitch can cross the punt threshold entirely from in-game decisions.

What Is a Punt Game?

A punt game is a major league game where a manager has made a deliberate, pre-meditated decision — before the first pitch is thrown, or through decisions made during the game — that winning this particular game is not the priority. It is a calculated concession: rest the stars, protect the arms, absorb the loss and prepare for what comes next.

No manager announces a punt. The box score won't say it. The broadcast rarely names it. But the evidence is always there — in the lineup card, the pitching selection, and the in-game decisions. The Punt Index exists to find that evidence and quantify it in real time. A punt game is still a punt game even if the team wins.

How the Score Works
Two Independent Tracks

Every game is scored on a 0–80 scale across two separate tracks: a pre-game score based on lineup and pitching decisions before first pitch, and an in-game score based on behavioral signals during play. They operate independently — a clean pre-game can still become a punt in-game.

Pre-Game Lock

A pre-game punt score of 46 or higher locks the game as a confirmed punt before first pitch. That verdict cannot be reversed — no matter what happens on the field. In-game behavior can add to the score, but cannot remove the flag.

Ace Disqualifier

If a team starts their ace — defined as FIP ≤ 3.40, or within 0.35 of that team's own season-best FIP — the pre-game score is immediately zeroed. A manager who sends their best arm is making a competitive statement. No lineup decision overrides that.

Live Team Quality

Context signals (fatigue, series position, back-to-backs) are scaled by each team's live punt motivation — derived entirely from current win%, run differential, and standings. No preseason labels. Teams earn their tier through actual performance, and it updates every time standings refresh.

Pre-Game Signals

These signals are evaluated before first pitch. Lineup signals are objective — the same formula applies to every team regardless of their record. Context signals (series position, fatigue, standings) are scaled by the team's live punt motivation, which is derived from their current win% and run differential.

Season Timing Multiplier

All scores are multiplied by a timing factor that reflects how much each game matters in the standings. September games are weighted at 100% — every game counts in a pennant race. Early season games are discounted because the standings context hasn't established itself yet.
