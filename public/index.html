Some Losses Are Planned

Not every loss is created equal. The Punt Index tracks when MLB managers make a deliberate pre-game decision to compromise their chances of winning, scoring and logging every instance across all 30 teams.

📋 The Definition

The decision is intentional and calculated, not a product of injury or circumstance, and is almost always made in the context of a series already clinched or a favorable upcoming schedule. A team throwing their ace cannot be punting — the starting pitcher is the clearest indicator of intent. Punt games are only tracked once a team has 60 stored games of lineup data — ensuring detection is based on real baselines, not assumptions.

⚙️ How It's Tracked

Every game is scored 0–10 using FIP-based pitching matchups, weighted lineup absences (adjusted for injuries and known platoons), series context, and team quality. One hard rule: if a team is starting their ace, the game cannot be a punt — full stop. Detection activates per team once 60 real games are stored — not based on the calendar date.

⚾ The Story Behind It

This started as a personal project from a lifelong Yankees fan who noticed something nobody was officially tracking — teams quietly giving away games that matter in the standings. The idea grew into a full analytics system covering all 30 teams equally. It's a fan-built, original stat with no official MLB equivalent. How It Works

The Punt Index pulls official data directly from MLB's own servers — the same source the league uses internally. Here's exactly what happens behind the scenes every time you analyze a date.

📡 The Data Source

Every lineup, every starting pitcher, every series position — it all comes from the official MLB Stats API, the same free public feed that powers most baseball apps and websites. This isn't scraped data or estimates. When you hit Refresh, the site pulls the real confirmed lineups for that day directly from MLB.

📊 Learning What's Normal

Each team starts in calibration mode. For the first 60 games, the site records who actually shows up in each team's batting order every night — building a real picture of who the everyday starters are, based on actual games rather than preseason assumptions. Once a team hits 60 stored games, that baseline is ready and punt detection turns on.

But the baseline doesn't freeze at game 60. It keeps updating throughout the season — and it weighs recent games more heavily than older ones. The last 20 games matter most, the 20 before that matter half as much, and anything older matters even less. This means the site automatically adapts to two things that happen constantly in baseball:

Trades: If a player was an everyday starter during calibration but gets traded in July, they stop showing up in lineups. Within a few weeks, their absence no longer counts as a signal — the algorithm has naturally moved on.

Injury returns: If an everyday player is returning from injury — such as Ketel Marte, Blake Snell, or Jackson Holliday — they may have missed the first 60 games entirely, so the baseline won't include them yet. But as they play game after game after returning, they earn their way into the baseline. By the time they've appeared in 14 of the last 20 games, their absence is treated as a meaningful signal — the same as any other established starter.

🔍 Spotting the Difference

Once a baseline exists, the algorithm compares each day's batting order against what's normal for that team. Not every absence counts equally. A player on the injured list carries zero weight — that's not a managerial decision. A day-to-day player carries partial weight. And a healthy everyday star sitting out for no stated reason carries full weight.

The starting pitcher is always the final check, tracked separately from the batting order. If a team is throwing their ace — defined as their best arm based on season stats — the game cannot be a punt, regardless of who's in the lineup. You don't concede a game when you're sending your best pitcher to the mound.

⚠️ What It Can't Do

The Punt Index is reactive, not predictive. It knows what happened on the days you analyze — it doesn't monitor the transaction wire in real time. If a player gets scratched an hour before first pitch and you haven't pulled that day's data yet, the site won't know until you do. Think of it as a daily audit tool, not a live injury tracker. Run it each day and the picture becomes complete.

🚫 Ace Disqualifier — Automatic Zero
If a team is starting their ace, the punt score is immediately set to 0 regardless of any other signals. Two conditions trigger this: the SP's FIP ≤ 3.50 (league-wide ace threshold), or the SP's FIP is within 0.40 of that team's own season-best (their relative ace). You don't punt when you're throwing your best arm.
